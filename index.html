ISSSE 2026


The 18th International Summer School on Software Engineering

University of Salerno, Italy

June 15-19, 2026

Navigate the Future of Software Engineering

Join ISSSE 2026 to explore how AI and agentic development are reshaping the field—and discover how to position your research in a rapidly evolving landscape.

Software engineering is at a crossroads. The rapid adoption of agentic development—AI systems that can plan and execute substantial parts of the software lifecycle—is reshaping how software is built, evaluated, and maintained. For PhD students and researchers, this is both an extraordinary moment of opportunity and a genuine source of uncertainty: never has it been easier to have impact, yet easier for a research direction to be disrupted by fast-moving tools, practices, and expectations.

In this rapidly evolving landscape, the 18th edition of the International Summer School on Software Engineering (ISSSE 2026) is designed to help you navigate this transformation.

This summer school brings together industry leaders and practitioners from major tech companies (including Google, Microsoft, and Meta) working on the technologies transforming software engineering today, alongside outstanding researchers—both established figures and rising stars in the software engineering community. You’ll hear the latest developments from industry and academia, and you’ll have opportunities to get feedback and guidance on your research ideas from experts at the forefront of this shift.

Whether you are exploring your research direction or looking to strengthen and reposition your work, ISSSE 2026 offers a unique opportunity to connect, learn, and grow within a vibrant international community.

And where better than the beautiful bay of Salerno for such an intellectually transformative experience?

Paul Baecke

Microsoft


From Autocomplete to Co-engineer: How LLMs Are Reshaping the Software Development Lifecycle


From Autocomplete to Co-engineer: How LLMs Are Reshaping the Software Development Lifecycle

Abstract. Large language models have moved far beyond code completion. In this talk, I reflect on fifteen years of building search and AI systems at Microsoft — from Bing and Windows Search to Microsoft Copilot — and how the introduction of LLMs into the development cycle has fundamentally changed the way engineers and applied scientists work. The shift is not merely one of speed. It is a shift in what the scarce resource actually is: no longer the writing of code, but the understanding of complex systems, the quality of specifications, and the design of validation loops. I will explore how practices such as spec-driven development, agentic inner-loop testing, and LLM-assisted evaluation are changing the daily rhythm of engineering teams, and what this means for the skills, workflows, and culture that high-performing software organisations need to cultivate. Drawing on concrete examples from building and shipping AI at scale, I will argue that the most important question is not how to make engineers faster, but how to redesign the development process itself for a world in which code is increasingly cheap and human attention is the binding constraint.

Bio. Paul Baecke is a Partner Group Engineering Manager at Microsoft AI, based in London. Over 15 years at Microsoft, he has worked at the intersection of search, AI, and productivity — beginning with Bing and Windows Search, where he helped build relevance and ranking systems at global scale, and progressing to his current role leading a team of over 100 engineers and scientists working on Microsoft 365 Copilot, Microsoft's flagship AI assistant and the centrepiece of its vision for the future of work. Paul's team is responsible for the product quality, evaluation, and post-training of models. He holds multiple granted US patents in search, machine learning, and intelligent interfaces. Paul holds degrees in Mathematics, Applied Mathematics and Computer Science from Rhodes University and Philosophy from Tilburg University.

Mark Harman

Meta & University College London


Just-in‑Time Testing: New on-the-fly test generation for an agentic world that prevents serious system failures

Just-in‑Time Testing: New on-the-fly test generation for an agentic world that prevents serious system failures

Abstract. This talk covers recent work on automated software testing at Meta, focussing on Just-in‑Time Tests (JiTTests).

JiTTests are a fundamentally novel approach to testing where tests are automatically generated by large language models (LLMs) on the fly to catch bugs – even ones that traditional testing might not catch – just-in-time before the code lands into production. A Catching JiTTest focuses specifically on finding regressions introduced by a code change. This type of testing reimagines decades of software testing theory and practice.

While traditional testing relies on static test suites, manual authoring, and ongoing maintenance, Catching JiTTests require no test maintenance and no test code review, meaning engineers can focus their expertise on real bugs, not false positives. Catching JiTTests use sophisticated techniques to maximize test signal value and minimize false positive drag, targeting test signals where they matter most: on serious failures.

Blog post: https://engineering.fb.com/2026/02/11/developer-tools/the-death-of-traditional-testing-agentic-development-jit-testing-revival/

arXiv version of forthcoming FSE 2026 paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.22832

Bio. Mark Harman is a Research Scientist at Meta London and a professor at University College London. He joined Meta following acquisition of his startup Majicke. He has published over 300 papers, with over 48,000 citations, and an H index of 111, making him the most highly cited scientist in the field of both Software Testing and of Program Analysis. His work has been deployed throughout Meta’s platforms for the past nine years, directly impacting over 3 billion people who rely on its product’s for social networking, community building and communication. His work has also directly impacted more than 200 million small companies that use Instagram, Facebook and WhatsApp to reach their customers and indirectly impacted many others that have deployed technology based on it, such as Microsoft, Google and Amazon. For his scientific work, Harman received the IEEE Harlan Mills Award and the ACM Outstanding Research Award in 2019. In 2020, he was elected a fellow of the Royal Academy of Engineering.

Michele Tufano

Google


Scaling Agentic Program Repair at Google

Scaling Agentic Program Repair at Google

Abstract. While Large Language Models and agentic AI systems have demonstrated remarkable success on open-source program repair benchmarks, deploying these systems in a complex, enterprise-scale environment introduces a new set of distinct challenges. This talk explores recent research from Google’s DevAI team on bridging the gap between academic automated program repair (APR) and practical industrial deployment.

We will explore the ecosystem of agentic bug-fixing, centered around Passerine, an agentic repair system designed to operate within a massive, multilingual monorepo.

The talk will cover several critical areas of our research, including: (i) examining how real-world enterprise bugs differ from standard open-source benchmarks (like SWE-Bench) and why they require tailored agentic approaches; (ii) enabling agents to autonomously generate Bug Reproduction Tests (BRTs) and the benefits of dynamically cogenerating the fix and the test in a single trajectory; (iii) combating developer review fatigue by applying bug abstention (filtering unfixable bugs prior to execution) and patch validation (rejecting unlikely fixes); and (iv) utilizing LLMs-as-a-Judge alongside human-refined rubrics to reliably and scalably evaluate patch validity.

This talk is designed for software engineering researchers and practitioners interested in applied AI, large language models, automated testing, and program repair. Attendees will leave with a holistic understanding of how agentic software engineering is evolving to handle real-world complexity, establish ground-truth evaluations, and respect the cognitive load of human reviewers.

Bio. Michele Tufano is a Sr. Software Engineer on the DevAI Research team at Google, where he focuses on the intersection of Artificial Intelligence and Software Engineering. His current work centers on designing agentic systems for automated program repair, bug reproduction testing, and LLM-driven development at an industrial scale. Prior to joining Google, Michele was a Research Scientist at Microsoft, continuing his research on AI for Code. He holds a Ph.D. from William & Mary, with a thesis focusing on Neural Machine Translation for automated bug-fixing.

Gabriele De Vito

UniSA


Advancing Automated Issue Report Classification with LLMs

Advancing Automated Issue Report Classification with LLMs

Abstract.

Issue reports, i.e., textual descriptions of bugs, feature requests, and other software-related issues, are a central artifact in modern software maintenance and are used to track, prioritize, and manage development tasks, as well as to support debugging, feature evolution, and team coordination. Classifying them according to their nature is both critical and time-consuming, and provides an ideal testbed for studying how LLMs perform in specialized text classification tasks, particularly given the challenges posed by long and often unstructured reports containing mixed information, the variability of model predictions across different runs and settings, and the need for outputs that are both reliable for practical use and interpretable by developers.

This talk will present novel methods to address these challenges grounded in two complementary perspectives: understanding user intent and example-guided decision-making. It will also introduce several methodological tools, including (i) a MapReduce-like approach to handle long reports and preserve relevant information, (ii) techniques for measuring LLM consistency across multiple runs and temperature settings with direct implications for reproducibility in empirical studies, and (iii) using perplexity as a confidence signal to trigger human review and develop more intelligent ensemble strategies. Along with exploring unexpected LLM behaviors and the unique misclassification causes in issue reports, the talk will offer specific recommendations for implementing these techniques in real- world scenarios where reliability, cost, and privacy are all important.

Bio. Gabriele De Vito is a postdoctoral researcher at the University of Salerno, where he received his PhD in Computer Science. With over twenty years of experience in software engineering, he has held roles including R&D Manager, CIO, and CTO, and has scientifically coordinated national and European-funded research projects with a total value exceeding €70 million. He has served on the program committees of international conferences (e.g., EASE, SEAA) and as a reviewer for software engineering journals (e.g., IEEE TSE). He co-chaired IWSM- MENSURA in 2023 and 2026. His work has been recognized with the COSMIC Research Award 2019. His research interests primarily lie in software engineering, machine learning, and large language models.

Fiorella Zampetti

UniSannio


Trustworthiness, legal and ethical aspects in AI-driven software engineering

Trustworthiness, legal and ethical aspects in AI-driven software engineering

Abstract.

The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) and autonomous agents into the software development lifecycle represents a fundamental shift in the socio technical landscape of software production. In this era, the traditional paradigms of quality and reliability are challenged by the broader concept of trustworthiness. This talk explores the multi-faceted challenges of AI-driven software engineering, examining the critical intersection of trustworthiness, legal liability, and ethics within AI-driven environments, moving beyond speculative benefits to analyze the empirical evidence of AI's impact on code quality and technical debt.

Bio. Fiorella Zampetti is an Assistant Professor at University of Sannio, Italy. She received her Ph.D. degree in Information Technologies for Engineering from the University of Sannio (Italy) in 2019. Her current research is focused on empirical software engineering, artificial intelligence for software engineering, DevOps for Cyber-Physical Systems (CPSs), software maintenance and evolution, and mining software repositories. She has served as a program committee member of international conferences in the software engineering field and she also serves as a reviewer for Software Engineering journals including TOSEM, TSE, EMSE, JSEP, and JSS. She is Program co-chair for the ICSME Visions and Emerging Results Track 2026 and also Program co-chair for TechDebt 2027. She is a member of the editorial boards of the Journal of Systems & Software and the Journal of Software: Evolution and Process, the Journal of Systems & Software, and Science of Computer Programming.

Student Talks Submission

In this year's edition, Ph.D. and undergraduate/graduate students can submit a talk for the ISSSE Student Talk Session to discuss ideas and gather feedback on ongoing research. ISSSE accepts presentation abstracts (max 2 pages) for review for relevance. ISSSE does not have proceedings, but the contributions will be available on this website. Presentation abstracts can be either novel research results or results that have already been published or are ready to be submitted to a conference or journal. Of particular interest are summaries that aim to provoke discussion, open new avenues for challenge, etc.
To prepare your talk to ISSSE, please adhere to this latex style, two-column style (sample-2col.tex and ceurart.cls), which is also available on Overleaf, and submit through the Easychair platform.
The deadline for student talk proposal is the April 26.

Venue and Accommodation

ISSSE will be held at the University of Salerno's computer science department, located a few kilometers from Salerno at the junction of motorway intersections—more details at https://web.unisa.it/en/home.

The best way to reach the campus from abroad is first to go to Salerno. The nearest International Airport is Naples Capodichino (code NAP). You can take a bus (Alibus) from the airport to the central station in Naples and get off here for connections to Salerno through high-speed and traditional train services.

Frequent buses connect the city to the university. Specifically, buses number 7 and 17 ("Linea 7" and "Linea 17" in Italian) arrive at the university from the Salerno train station (Salerno FS).
More information on the bus route can be found on the main page of Busitalia.
A dedicated app, QuiBus Campania, is available for Android and iOS and allows rapid individuation of available routes. The app can be downloaded for Android or iOS (Italian language only!).

Salerno is located about 60 km south of Naples and is considered a gateway to the Amalfi Coast. It offers much, from a medieval city center that takes you back to its ancient and prestigious origins to a seafront with modern and avant-garde architecture.
